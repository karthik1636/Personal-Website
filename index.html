Projects

Multivariate Analysis of Suicide Rates: Socioeconomic Factors and Temporal Trends

This project delves into the statistical complexities of suicide rates across various demographics, utilizing a sophisticated array of statistical techniques to decipher trends and patterns. Employing the R programming language, the study integrates multiple statistical libraries and frameworks such as ggplot2 for data visualization, dplyr for data manipulation, and car and MVN for robust statistical testing.

Key methods include multivariate analysis of variance (MANOVA) to test differences across groups, Exploratory Data Analysis (EDA) to inspect, clean, and prepare data, and multiple regression analysis to understand the impact of various factors on suicide rates. Techniques such as non-parametric tests were used to confirm findings in non-normal data distributions, showcasing a comprehensive approach to addressing complex sociological phenomena through quantitative analysis.

The outcome of this project provides insights into the socio-economic and demographic factors influencing suicide rates, offering a valuable resource for public health policy makers to develop targeted interventions.

Advanced Predictive Modeling for Diabetes Risk Assessment and Prevention Strategies

Our project utilized advanced machine learning techniques to predict diabetes risk among individuals. We employed logistic regression models to analyze the likelihood of developing diabetes based on a dataset sourced from Kaggle, which included critical predictors such as age, BMI, blood glucose levels, and lifestyle factors.

We approached the problem by first performing a thorough Exploratory Data Analysis (EDA) to understand the correlations and distributions of various predictors. Key techniques included oversampling with the ROSE library to balance the dataset, utilizing chi-squared tests for feature selection, and Bayesian Information Criteria (BIC) for model validation.

Our findings revealed significant predictors such as hypertension and BMI, which were intricately modeled to predict diabetes risk. We validated these models on a testing set to ensure robustness and accuracy. The final model not only predicts diabetes but also aids in understanding the impact of various health indicators on the disease's onset.

This project underscores the importance of machine learning in healthcare, providing insights that can lead to better preventative health measures and management of diabetes, aligning with our goal to leverage data science for impactful health outcomes.

Comparative Analysis of Bidirectional LSTM and Transformer Models for Emotion Detection

This project investigates the efficacy of Bidirectional LSTM (Long Short-Term Memory) and Transformer architectures in the domain of emotion analysis within natural language processing. The objective was to determine which model better handles the nuances of human emotion in textual data.

We commenced with a foundational Exploratory Data Analysis (EDA) to gauge the distribution of emotional expressions across the dataset. Subsequent modeling involved implementing a Bidirectional LSTM, known for its ability to capture context from both past and future data points in a sequence, and a Transformer, which utilizes self-attention mechanisms to process sequences in parallel.

The methodologies employed included tokenization and padding of text data to uniform sequence lengths, essential for processing by our neural network models. Both models were trained and their performance metrics such as precision, recall, and F1-score were critically evaluated. The Transformer model, with its innovative attention mechanism, was hypothesized to outperform the LSTM in capturing contextual nuances.

Our findings illustrated that while both models achieved high validation accuracies, the Transformer model demonstrated superior efficiency in training time and scalability, making it a preferable choice for real-time emotion analysis applications.

Wealth, Health, and Pollution Analysis: An Interactive Tableau Story

This Tableau dashboard delves into the intricate relationships between a country's wealth (GDP), health outcomes (life expectancy), and environmental impact (pollution levels). By aggregating data from the World Bank, WHO, and the World Inequality Database, we provide a multi-faceted view of how economic factors correlate with public health and environmental conditions.

Our analysis covers key questions such as the correlation between GDP and life expectancy, the health implications of pollution levels, and the economic impact of environmental policies like the Paris Agreement. Interactive visualizations allow users to explore trends over time, compare different countries, and understand the broader socioeconomic implications.

The dashboard is designed to be intuitive, offering users the ability to filter data by country, year, and economic indicators. It highlights the dual impact of economic development on public health and the environment, providing valuable insights for policymakers, researchers, and the public.

This project not only sheds light on the often complex interdependencies between wealth, health, and pollution but also serves as a tool for education and decision-making, promoting a deeper understanding of global challenges.
